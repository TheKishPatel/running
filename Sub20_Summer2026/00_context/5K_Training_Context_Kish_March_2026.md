## Training Principles & Coaching Preferences

### Coaching Style Preferences

**Direct, analytical approach:**
- Data-driven decisions using HR, pace, power, and split analysis
- No flattery or excessive praise (avoid "you're absolutely right", "that's a deep question")
- Honest assessments even when harsh
- Evidence-based pacing - all training paces justified by recent performance data
- Precise target setting (e.g., 4:15/km) rather than vague ranges

**Communication style:**
- Concise when appropriate, detailed when analyzing sessions
- Focus on actionable insights
- Conservative progression with aggressive adaptation based on breakthrough performances
- Smart recovery management factoring in illness, alcohol, sleep, life stress

**Analysis depth:**
- Detailed split analysis for key sessions
- HR response and drift examination
- Comparison of perceived effort to objective data
- Consistency tracking across intervals
- Recovery monitoring between efforts
- External stressor impact assessment

### Core Training Philosophy

**HR primacy over pace:**
- When pace and HR conflict, HR data determines effort zones
- Example: 5:30/km @ 165 BPM = too hard, regardless of "easy pace" label
- Example: 4:24/km @ 168 BPM better than 4:21/km @ 175 BPM for same session

**Conservative starts, aggressive adaptation:**
- Start new phases conservatively
- Rapidly adjust targets based on breakthrough performances
- Example: After Jan 24 breakthrough (4:24 tempo), immediately adjusted future targets

**Smart recovery management:**
- Complete rest at first illness symptom (learned from August/November patterns)
- Move sessions when sleep-deprived (demonstrated multiple times)
- Factor alcohol impact into planning (2 drinks OK, 4+ compromises 2-3 days)
- Barry's Bootcamp counts as hard session in weekly load

**Phase-appropriate training:**
- Week 1-2: Very conservative, focus on consistency
- Week 3-4: Slight pace progression if HR response is good
- Week 5+: More aggressive progression based on adaptation
- Red flags: HR drift >10%, excessive fatigue, pace regression

### Discipline Patterns & Management

**Identified patterns:**
1. **Pace pushing when feeling good:** Prescribed 5:50, runs 5:30
2. **Environmental adversity response:** Rain/dark/cold → pushes to "get it done"
3. **Barry's effort:** Historically 8-9/10 when prescribed 6-7/10 (improved by Week 9)
4. **Race starts:** Tendency to go out too fast (Parkrun 4:04 vs prescribed 4:08)

**Successful interventions:**
1. **Trail running:** Norda 001s + trails = natural pace governor (Dec 15 success)
2. **HR monitoring:** Focus on HR targets rather than pace targets
3. **Conservative race pacing:** Learned from Parkrun error, executed perfectly March 8
4. **Barry's recalibration:** Understanding 6/10 means HR data shows Zone 2-3 majority

**Alcohol impact:**
- 1-2 drinks: Minimal impact on next session
- 4+ drinks: Compromised training for 2-3 days
- Examples: Week 2 (4 drinks Friday → compromised Sunday), Week 6 (8 drinks Saturday → correctly skipped Sunday)

**Sleep requirements:**
- Poor sleep → move session to next day (correct decision demonstrated multiple times)
- 8+ hours optimal for quality sessions
- Daughter waking = compromised sleep = adjust training accordingly

### Illness Management Protocol

**"Neck check" rule:**
- Symptoms above neck (runny nose, sneezing): May train easy
- Symptoms below neck (chest congestion, cough, phlegm): Complete rest
- Any fever: Complete rest
- Sore throat: Complete rest (leads to respiratory issues)

**Aggressive rest approach:**
- First symptom → immediate complete rest
- Don't "test" with easy run until 100% symptom-free
- Better to rest 5 extra days than train too soon and extend illness 3 weeks
- Learned from August 2025 and November 2025 patterns

**Return from illness:**
1. 100% symptom-free for 24 hours minimum
2. Test run: 3-4km @ 6:00/km, HR <145 BPM
3. If symptoms return during or after test → rest another 2-3 days
4. If test run clean → easy running for 3-5 days before resuming quality sessions
5. Rebuild tempo work before intervals

3. **No "testing" with easy runs** - wait until 100% clear

**What doesn't work:**
1. **Training through symptoms** - Feb 21 attempt made phlegm worse
2. **"Just easy running"** - still stresses immune system

**Return protocol:**
1. Symptom-free 24+ hours
2. Test run: 3-4km @ 6:00/km, HR <145 BPM
3. No symptoms during or 2 hours after test run
4. If clear: 3-5 days easy running before quality sessions
5. Rebuild tempo before intervals
6. Monitor for any symptom return

**Immune suppression factors:**
1. **Alcohol:** 4+ drinks compromises immune system
2. **Sleep deprivation:** <6 hours increases illness risk
3. **Overtraining:** Pushing pace when prescribed easy
4. **Life stress:** Work travel, family commitments

**Prevention strategies:**
1. Zero alcohol during key training blocks
2. Prioritize 8+ hours sleep (negotiate with partner for daughter duties)
3. Strict discipline on easy pace adherence
4. Rest day after high-stress periods

- 5-10% drift = acceptable for longer efforts
- >10% drift = aerobic base needs work or pushing too hard

## Red Flags & Warning Signs

### Training Red Flags

**Immediate session abort:**
- Any coughing during run
- Breathing restriction or wheezing
- Chest pain or tightness
- Unusual fatigue within first km
- Dizziness or lightheadedness

**Concerning patterns (adjust training):**
- HR drift >10% during tempo runs
- Cannot maintain target pace despite high HR
- Recovery HR staying >165 BPM for 2+ minutes post-interval
- Morning resting HR >60 BPM for 3+ consecutive days
- Persistent muscle soreness >48 hours

**Pace discipline failures:**
- Consistently running 15+ seconds/km faster than prescribed easy pace
- Starting intervals faster than target pace
- Racing training partners on easy runs
- Pushing pace in adverse weather to "get it done"

### Illness Warning Signs

**Early intervention required:**
- Scratchy throat (even mild)
- Unusual fatigue despite adequate sleep
- Elevated morning resting HR (>5 BPM above baseline)
- Reduced appetite
- Mood changes (irritability, low motivation)

**Immediate complete rest:**
- Sore throat (any severity)
- Congestion or runny nose with colored mucus
- Any cough or phlegm
- Fever (any temperature elevation)
- Muscle aches unrelated to training
- Swollen lymph nodes

### Overtraining Indicators

**Performance markers:**
- Same pace feels harder (elevated HR)
- Target paces becoming unsustainable
- Recovery between intervals inadequate
- Long runs feeling unusually difficult
- Race performance declining

**Physiological markers:**
- Morning HR elevated >5 BPM for 1+ week
- HR during easy runs consistently >10 BPM above normal
- Sleep disturbances (difficulty falling/staying asleep)
- Increased minor illnesses
- Persistent fatigue

**Behavioral markers:**
- Reduced motivation to train
- Irritability or mood swings
- Anxiety about training
- Loss of appetite
- Training feeling like obligation vs. enjoyment

### Recovery Assessment

**Well recovered:**
- Morning HR at or below baseline
- Motivation high for session
- Previous session felt controlled
- Sleep quality good (7-8+ hours)
- No lingering muscle soreness

**Adequate recovery:**
- Morning HR 2-3 BPM above baseline
- Motivation moderate
- Some residual fatigue but manageable
- Sleep adequate (6-7 hours)
- Mild muscle soreness

**Insufficient recovery:**
- Morning HR >5 BPM above baseline
- Low motivation, dreading session
- Significant fatigue from previous sessions
- Poor sleep (<6 hours)
- Persistent muscle soreness
- **Action: Move quality session or replace with easy run**
